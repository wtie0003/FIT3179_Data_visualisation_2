Global CO2 Emissions: Trends and Sources

Introduction

CO2 emissions is a critical issue as several nations face the environmental severe impact of industrialization and energy use. This visualization explores global emission trends, focusing on how major countries such as Australia, China, and the USA contribute to climate change. By examining these emissions, we highlight the urgent need for sustainable solutions to address the growing challenge of global warming.

Global CO2 Emissions by Country(1960-2021)

*click the slider to change year

This interactive map shows the global distribution of CO2 emissions from 1960 to 2021, measured in Million Metric Tons (Mt). Industrialized nations like China, the USA, and Russia are the largest contributors, while emissions remain lower across much of Africa and South America.
As time progresses, the map highlights rising emissions, especially in rapidly developing economies such as China. The color scale visualizes these changes, with the colour scale (viridis) moving towards yellow, indicating higher CO2 outputs, reflecting the increasing environmental impact of industrial activities worldwide.

Major Contributors to CO2 Emissions

This stacked bar chart shows global CO2 emissions by the source of CO2 emissions from 2000 to 2021, broken down into major contributors such as coal, oil, gas, cement, and others. Each bar represents the total emissions for a given year, with segments showing each source’s contribution.
The chart highlights the dominance of fossil fuels, especially coal and gas, in driving emissions. While overall emissions have steadily increased, there are signs of shifts toward cleaner energy in recent years. Users can interact with the chart to explore specific sources and time periods, offering valuable insights into how industrial activities and energy consumption impact the environment.This visualization helps analysts, policymakers, and industries track key emission trends and work toward more sustainable solutions.

Coal Emissions by Country (1960-2021)

This line chart illustrates coal-related CO2 emissions from 1960 to 2021 across six major coal producers: Australia, China, India, Russia, South Africa, and Indonesia. The chart highlights coal’s role as a key energy source, focusing on Australia’s contribution to global emissions.
While Australia is known for its vast coal reserves and reliance on coal-fired power plants, its emissions are still far below those of China, which has seen a rapid increase in coal consumption since the early 2000s. China’s exponential rise now makes it the dominant source of global coal emissions, overshadowing other countries.
This chart allows for comparisons between Australia and other nations. India shows steady growth, while Russia’s emissions remain relatively stable. Despite Australia being a top coal exporter, recent years show slight efforts toward a transition to cleaner energy sources. This visualization highlights Australia’s role in global coal emissions and underscores the need for strategies to balance economic growth with environmental responsibility.

Australia’s CO2 Emissions in Global Context

This bubble chart shows the relationship between CO2 emissions per capita and total emissions for various countries in 2021. Each bubble's size represents total emissions, with Australia highlighted in green. This chart compares both per capita emissions and the total emissions. Australia’s CO2 emissions per capita are over 15 metric tons, making it one of the top emitters per person, though its total emissions are much lower than global leaders like China, the USA, and India. China leads with over 11,000 Mt, while Australia is around 391 Mt.

Top Emitters

This heatmap highlights per capita CO2 emissions of the top four emitters — Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Brunei — compared to Australia and the USA from 2010 to 2021.Unlike the previous bubble chart, which compared total vs. per capita emissions, this heatmap focuses solely on per capita emissions. Qatar leads with the highest per capita emissions, followed by Kuwait and Bahrain. Despite its lower total emissions, Australia still ranks high in per capita emissions, surpassing the USA. This visualization highlights the impact of smaller, energy-intensive countries and Australia’s ongoing challenge in managing its carbon footprint.
